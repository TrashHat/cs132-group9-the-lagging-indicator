Model Selection & Methodology

Prophet[1] (Taylor & Letham, 2017), developed by Meta, was selected as the forecasting model over simpler regression-based approaches. Three properties of the PSE sector data made it the appropriate choice.

  • 1
    Structural break handling The dataset contains a clearly identifiable regime change in March 2020, where index values collapsed sharply before entering a multi-year recovery. Prophet models trend as piecewise linear segments with automatically detected changepoints, representing this break explicitly rather than imposing a single global curve.
  • 2
    No stationarity requirement Unlike ARIMA, Prophet does not require the series to be stationary, which is appropriate here since each sector's normalized index exhibits a clear directional trend over the recovery period.
  • 3
    Calibrated uncertainty intervals Prophet produces forecast uncertainty intervals via Laplace approximation on trend noise, communicating a range of plausible future trajectories rather than a single point estimate.

Key Finding

Holding Firms (PSHO) is the only sector with a positive R² (+0.0936), reflecting its compressed, plateau-like trajectory. Five of six sectors returned negative R², driven by externally-triggered surges in 2024–2025 that no univariate trend model could anticipate.

The strongly negative R² values for Industrial (−2.9083), Services (−2.3220), and Mining & Oil (−1.2716) reflect the commodity price surge and demand recovery shocks in 2024 to 2025, externally driven movements that a model trained on 2019 to 2023 trend data cannot anticipate. This is a constraint of all univariate trend-based forecasting methods, not a failure of model specification.

Forecast Findings & SDG 8 Implications
Finding F1: Property

Property (PSPR) is not projected to reach its pre-pandemic baseline by December 2026 under the model's central estimate, extending the EDA finding that PSPR had not recovered as of April 2025.

This carries direct implications for SDG 8. The property sector employs significant portions of the construction and property management workforce in the Philippines. A sustained multi-year deficit below the 2019 baseline signals that capital allocation toward these industries remains structurally depressed, with employment and investment conditions likely to continue lagging the broader market recovery through at least 2026.

Finding F2: Persistent Divergence

Sector divergence is projected to persist through 2026. Mining & Oil remains elevated; Services trends moderately upward; Banks, Holding Firms, and Industrial cluster in the 80–120 range.

The forecast trajectories confirm that the sectoral gap identified in the EDA phase is not a transient condition. The Philippine equity market's post-pandemic configuration appears to be a structural feature of the recovery, with no convergence to a uniform sector level projected within the forecast window.

Among the individual sectors, Mining & Oil (PSMO) is projected to sustain the highest normalized level through 2026, though with the widest uncertainty interval, reflecting the commodity-price dependency of its recent gains. Services (PSSE) shows a moderate upward trajectory, consistent with continued recovery in telecommunications and retail demand. Banks (PSFI), Holding Firms (PSHO), and Industrial (PSIN) cluster tightly in the 80 to 120 range throughout the forecast window, indicating a recovery that has stabilized but not accelerated. Property (PSPR) remains the outlier, with its central forecast trajectory staying below the pre-pandemic baseline of 100 through December 2026. It is the sole sector for which the model projects no recovery completion within the combined observation and forecast window.

Limitations & Future Extensions
  • L1
    No exogenous variables Prophet does not incorporate interest rates, commodity prices, exchange rates, or geopolitical events, which are the primary drivers of short-term sector performance. The negative R² values on the test set for most sectors directly reflect this constraint.
  • L2
    Widening uncertainty at longer horizons Forecast uncertainty intervals widen considerably over the 2025 to 2026 window. The central forecast line should be interpreted as a conditional projection assuming no major structural shocks, not as a precise prediction of future index values.
  • L3
    Potential extensions Incorporating macroeconomic covariates (Philippine GDP growth rate, BSP policy rate, global commodity price indices) as Prophet regressors, or applying gradient-boosted tree ensembles with lagged features, would substantially improve predictive accuracy for commodity-linked sectors such as Mining & Oil.
Conclusion

This project examined whether the Philippine Stock Exchange's post-COVID recovery was sector-inclusive, analyzing daily closing prices for all six PSE sector indices from January 2019 to April 2025 and generating Prophet forecasts through December 2026.

The evidence is unambiguous: the recovery was not inclusive. Mining & Oil (PSMO) exceeded 250% of its January 2019 baseline by April 2025, driven by the global commodity price cycle, while Property (PSPR) had not returned to that same baseline after six years. The Kruskal-Wallis test (H = 5289.54, p < 0.001) confirms that the divergence in recovery trajectories across all six sectors is statistically significant, with all sector pairs except Holding Firms and Industrial showing meaningfully distinct paths. The Prophet forecast projects that this divergence persists through December 2026, with Property (PSPR) the sole sector projected to remain below its pre-pandemic baseline at the end of the forecast window.

These findings are directly relevant to SDG 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth. A fractured recovery, where gains are concentrated in commodity-linked equities while the property and construction sector remains structurally depressed, implies that the economic benefits of the post-pandemic period were unevenly distributed across the industries and workers that depend on each sector. The lagging indicator lived up to its name: the stock market reflected the uneven nature of the recovery, but only after the fact.

For investors, the findings suggest that sector-level diversification matters: allocating across all six PSE sectors does not guarantee equal exposure to recovery upside, given how concentrated the post-pandemic gains were in Mining and Oil. For policymakers and labor advocates, the persistent underperformance of the Property sector carries a more urgent signal. The construction and real estate workforce has been operating in a structurally depressed capital environment for over six years, and the forecast offers no relief within the current window. Addressing the conditions that continue to suppress property investment, whether through targeted fiscal incentives, infrastructure spending, or housing policy reform, is a concrete step toward the inclusive economic growth that SDG 8 calls for.
