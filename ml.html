Model Selection & Methodology

Prophet (Taylor & Letham, 2017), developed by Meta, was selected as the forecasting model for this analysis over simpler regression-based approaches for three principal reasons. First, Prophet is specifically designed for business time series that exhibit non-linear trends and structural breaks. The PSE sector index data contains a clearly identifiable regime change at the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in March 2020, where index values collapsed sharply before entering a multi-year recovery period. Prophet models the trend as a series of piecewise linear segments with automatically detected changepoints, allowing it to represent this structural break explicitly rather than imposing a single global polynomial curve across the entire observation window.

Second, unlike autoregressive models such as ARIMA, Prophet does not require the series to be stationary, which is appropriate for this dataset since each sector's normalized index exhibits a clear directional trend over the recovery period. Third, Prophet produces calibrated forecast uncertainty intervals via a Laplace approximation on trend noise, which is more informative for policymakers and investors than point estimates alone. The uncertainty intervals communicate the range of plausible future trajectories under the model's assumptions, rather than presenting a single projected value as if it were certain.

Hyperparameter Tuning

The principal hyperparameter tuned was changepoint_prior_scale (CPS), which controls the flexibility of the piecewise trend. A low CPS value produces a smoother, more regularized trend that resists overfitting to short-term volatility. A high CPS value allows the trend to adapt rapidly to recent data at the cost of increased sensitivity to noise. Six candidate values were evaluated via grid search (0.001, 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5), with the value minimizing test-set RMSE selected independently for each sector.

Seasonality & Split

Weekly seasonality was disabled because PSE trading gaps on weekends are structural absences rather than seasonal patterns. Yearly seasonality was enabled to capture annual cycles related to fiscal reporting periods and macroeconomic budget cycles. The model was trained on January 2019 to December 2023, covering the pre-pandemic baseline, the COVID-19 crash, and the initial recovery. The held-out test set spans January 2024 to April 2025. Forecasts are generated for May 2025 to December 2026.

Model Performance: Test Set Evaluation

The table below presents the test-set performance of each sector's Prophet model evaluated across three metrics: Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and the coefficient of determination (R²). RMSE and MAE measure the average magnitude of prediction error in normalized index units. R² measures the proportion of variance in the test data explained by the model, where 1.0 indicates a perfect fit and values below 0 indicate that the model performs worse than a naive mean prediction.

The results reveal significant variation in predictive accuracy across sectors. Holding Firms (PSHO) is the only sector with a positive R² (0.0936), indicating that its compressed, plateau-like trajectory is the most predictable among the six. Banks and Financial (PSFI) and Property (PSPR) record the least negative R² values at -0.2342 and -0.2856 respectively, suggesting that their relatively stable trends over the test period were partially captured by the model.

The strongly negative R² values for Industrial (PSIN, -2.9083), Services (PSSE, -2.3220), and Mining and Oil (PSMO, -1.2716) indicate that the model substantially underestimated the actual performance of these sectors during the 2024 to 2025 test period. This outcome is attributable to the PSMO commodity price surge and the PSSE demand recovery, both of which represent externally-driven shocks that are structurally unpredictable from historical trend data alone. A trend-based model trained on 2019 to 2023 data, when PSMO was trading between 80 and 130 on the normalized scale, could not have anticipated the commodity-driven surge to values exceeding 200 in 2024 to 2026. This outcome reflects a fundamental constraint of all trend-based forecasting methods when applied to financially volatile, commodity-linked sectors rather than a failure of model specification.

Forecast Findings & SDG 8 Implications
F1 Property is not projected to reach its pre-pandemic baseline by December 2026

The forecast for the period from May 2025 to December 2026 yields one principal finding of direct relevance to the project's research questions and to SDG 8. Property (PSPR) is not projected to reach its pre-pandemic baseline of 100 within the forecast window under the model's central estimate. This extends the finding from the EDA phase, which established that PSPR had not recovered as of April 2025, and provides a quantitative projection confirming that recovery is not imminent under current trend conditions.

This finding carries direct implications for SDG 8: Decent Work and Economic Growth. The property sector encompasses real estate developers, REITs, and property management companies, which are significant employers in the Philippine construction and services industries. A sustained multi-year deficit below the pre-pandemic baseline indicates that capital allocation toward this sector has remained structurally depressed relative to 2019 levels. For policymakers evaluating whether economic growth is genuinely inclusive, the Property sector's projected non-recovery within the 2025 to 2026 window suggests that employment and investment conditions in construction-related industries may continue to lag behind the broader market recovery.

F2 Sector divergence is projected to persist through 2026

Mining and Oil (PSMO) is projected to maintain elevated levels through 2026, though the model's wide uncertainty interval for this sector reflects the externally-driven, commodity-linked nature of its gains. Services (PSSE) shows a moderate upward trajectory in the forecast, consistent with the continued normalization of consumer activity in the post-pandemic period. Banks and Financial, Holding Firms, and Industrial are projected to remain clustered in the 80 to 120 normalized index range, reflecting the partial but incomplete recoveries observed in the EDA analysis. Collectively, the forecast trajectories indicate that the sectoral divergence identified in the EDA phase is not a transient condition but a persistent structural feature of the Philippine equity market's post-pandemic configuration.

Limitations & Future Extensions

Several limitations of this analysis warrant explicit acknowledgment. Prophet is a trend-and-seasonality decomposition model and does not incorporate exogenous variables such as interest rates, commodity prices, exchange rates, or geopolitical events. These factors are primary drivers of short-term sector index performance in practice, and their exclusion means the model cannot anticipate event-driven movements in sectors like Mining and Oil and Services. The negative R² values on the test set for the majority of sectors reflect this constraint directly: the 2024 to 2025 period was characterized by externally-driven shocks that no univariate trend model could have captured.

Furthermore, the forecast uncertainty intervals widen considerably over the 2025 to 2026 horizon, reinforcing that longer-horizon forecasts carry substantially greater uncertainty. Investors and policymakers should interpret the central forecast line as a conditional projection that assumes no major structural shocks occur during the forecast window, rather than as a precise prediction of future index values.

A natural extension of this work would be to incorporate macroeconomic covariates as additional regressors within a Prophet or vector autoregression (VAR) framework, which would allow the model to condition its forecasts on observed economic indicators such as the Philippine GDP growth rate, the BSP policy rate, and global commodity price indices. Sector-specific models that explicitly account for commodity price cycles would substantially improve the predictive accuracy for Mining and Oil. Additionally, applying machine learning ensemble methods such as gradient-boosted trees with lagged features could capture the non-linear interactions between sectors that a univariate time series model cannot represent.
