Background & Motivation

In March 2020, the Philippine Stock Exchange Index (PSEi) collapsed by nearly 40% in a matter of weeks, marking one of the steepest single-event crashes in its recorded history. The COVID-19 pandemic simultaneously disrupted economic activity across all sectors of the economy, as community quarantine restrictions caused businesses to shutter, restricted mobility, and eroded investor confidence significantly.

However, not all sectors were affected equally, and the subsequent recovery did not proceed at the same pace across the board. Property and Services industries faced prolonged exposure to lockdown conditions due to their reliance on physical consumer activity, while Financial and Holding Firms companies were partially insulated by their diversified asset bases. By 2022 to 2023, several sectors had recovered to pre-pandemic index levels while others remained considerably below their historical highs.

This uneven recovery is directly relevant to SDG 8 (Decent Work and Economic Growth), which calls for sustained, inclusive economic growth and productive employment. The stock market, as a forward-looking indicator of economic expectations, reflects how capital allocators assess the long-term viability of each sector. Understanding which sectors recovered, and how quickly, provides a data-driven basis for evaluating where the Philippine economy has regained footing and where structural vulnerabilities persist.

This project uses the six official PSE sector indices to trace the full arc of the COVID-19 economic shock, from the initial crash through the recovery period up to April 2025. The central question is whether the post-pandemic recovery of the Philippine economy has been genuinely broad-based or concentrated in select sectors, which has direct implications for inclusive growth under SDG 8.

Research Questions
  • RQ1
    Crash & Recovery Which PSE sector experienced the steepest decline during the COVID-19 crash (Q1–Q2 2020), and which sector demonstrated the fastest recovery to pre-pandemic index levels?
  • RQ2
    Trajectory Differences Do the six PSE sector indices show statistically significant differences in their post-pandemic recovery trajectories from 2020 to 2025?
Research Hypotheses
  • H1
    Sector-Specific Crash & Recovery The Services and Property sectors experienced the steepest decline during Q1–Q2 2020 due to their high exposure to physical mobility and consumer activity, while the Industrial and Holding Firms sectors demonstrated the fastest recovery to pre-pandemic index levels.
  • H2
    Significant Trajectory Differences There is a statistically significant difference in post-COVID recovery trajectories across the six PSE sector indices from 2020 to 2025 (α = 0.05).
Objectives
  • O1
    To compare the magnitude and speed of sector-level index decline and recovery across the COVID-19 crash period (2019–2025) using daily PSE sector index closing price data.
  • O2
    To determine whether statistically significant differences exist in the post-COVID recovery trajectories of the six PSE sector indices using non-parametric hypothesis testing (Kruskal-Wallis test).
Proposed Solution

This project applies an Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) approach to the daily closing prices of all six PSE sector indices from January 2019 to April 2025. The analysis is structured around two sequential phases, each corresponding to one of the project's research questions.

Phase 1: Descriptive and Visual Analysis (RQ1). The first phase involves computing the percentage drawdown from each sector's pre-crash peak to its trough during Q1 to Q2 of 2020, as well as measuring the time elapsed for each sector to return to its pre-pandemic closing level. The results are visualized through a multi-line time series chart and a comparative bar chart of drawdown magnitude and recovery duration across sectors.

Phase 2: Statistical Hypothesis Testing (RQ2). The second phase applies the Kruskal-Wallis test, a non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA, to test whether the distributions of normalized index returns across the six sectors differ significantly during the recovery period from 2020 to 2025. This test is selected because financial return data is typically non-normal in distribution. If the test yields a statistically significant result at the 0.05 significance level, post-hoc pairwise comparisons using Dunn's test will identify which specific sector pairs exhibit the greatest divergence in recovery trajectories.
